# Launch resilience and team safety

Use this module when a feature is contentious, changes user control or market power, has known negative positions, may attract coordinated hostility, or when team members are being targeted.

This module separates three things that must not be conflated:

1. **Substantive criticism:** preserve, understand, and answer it.
2. **Disruptive but non-threatening behavior:** moderate consistently so useful discussion can continue.
3. **Harassment, hate, doxxing, or credible threats:** protect people; no one owes personal engagement.

The goal is not to silence criticism or manipulate coverage. It is to prevent foreseeable surprise, keep technical feedback usable, and protect the people doing the work.

## Before launch

- Name a launch/incident lead, backup, moderator(s), spokesperson, product/engineering decision owner, and routes to communications, security, HR/people support, and qualified legal counsel.
- Agree severity levels and what triggers extra moderation, a response, channel locking, a launch pause, rollback, or emergency escalation.
- Publish contribution and moderation expectations before controversy. Enable reporting and ensure they can be enforced.
- Choose one maintained public source of truth for status, evidence, known concerns, decisions, and updates.
- Route technical feedback through structured templates and dedicated issues instead of personal accounts.
- Prepare an FAQ that includes strongest objections, negative positions, unknowns, user costs, mitigations, and what would change the plan.
- Prepare truthful hold, correction, narrowing, delay, and rollback language.
- Minimize unnecessary personal details in staff pages, commits, presentations, issue templates, and launch materials. Offer voluntary digital-safety review; never make the targeted person responsible for organizational risk.
- Ask each public-facing team member what support and exposure level they want. Do not assume everyone should be a spokesperson.
- Schedule moderator coverage and handoffs; no individual should monitor hostile channels continuously.

## Monitor without feeding abuse

Track separately:

- Standards/design criticism and evidence gaps
- Product/implementation bugs
- Press narratives and factual errors
- Developer confusion/support issues
- Coordinated disruption/spam
- Harassment/hate
- Doxxing/private-information exposure
- Credible physical or cyber threats

Do not quote or redistribute doxxing content in normal reports. Store sensitive evidence only in approved restricted systems. Aggregate public reporting to protect targeted people.

## Response protocol

1. **Check immediate safety privately.** Contact the affected person discreetly, listen, and include them in decisions that affect them.
2. **Reduce exposure.** Reassign monitoring/public response, rotate spokespeople, and make disengagement explicit and consequence-free.
3. **Preserve evidence safely.** Capture timestamps, URLs, screenshots/messages, and chronology before content disappears; restrict access.
4. **Remove/report.** Use platform privacy-removal, abuse-reporting, blocking, comment minimization/deletion, and temporary/permanent conversation locks consistently with published policy.
5. **Escalate credible threats.** Use company security and qualified internal channels; emergency or law-enforcement involvement depends on immediacy, jurisdiction, organizational policy, and the targeted person's safety/preferences where possible.
6. **Maintain a safe feedback route.** If a public thread is locked, provide a structured moderated channel for technical criticism. Never label all negative feedback “abuse.”
7. **Correct facts and update decisions.** Respond from the team/source-of-truth account, not by sending an individual into repeated debate.
8. **Change the product or launch when warranted.** Communications cannot repair an unsafe design, misleading claim, or broken process.
9. **Aftercare.** Continue private check-ins, workload relief, security support, and a blameless retrospective after the acute incident. Agree a time-bounded follow-up cadence with affected people (for example daily during an acute period, then weekly while risk remains), recurrence triggers, owners, and privacy-preserving measures; do not impose monitoring that prolongs exposure.

## Failure conditions

The plan fails if it:

- Tells an individual engineer to debate harassers or monitor abuse alone
- Treats threats/doxxing as useful product feedback
- Hides legitimate technical criticism under a broad “toxicity” label
- Republishes personal information or sensitive evidence
- Makes launch optics more important than safety
- Assumes locking a thread resolves the underlying product concern
- Disciplines or blames a person for being targeted
- Publicly discloses an affected person's actions, location, or support choices without consent

## Output

- Risk and controversy assessment
- Roles/on-call/escalation matrix
- Public source-of-truth and channel plan
- Moderation and structured-feedback plan
- Prepared response/hold/correction/rollback materials
- Privacy and digital-safety checklist
- Incident log location and access policy
- Team support and handoff plan
- Post-incident learning review with follow-up cadence, recurrence alerts, owners, outcomes, and stop conditions
